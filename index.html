Correlation Graph

project digital footprint · Boxel Studio
Making the
invisible visible

Every VFX production generates a constant stream of signals — emails, Slack messages, ShotGrid status changes, filesystem events, render logs. In isolation, each signal is noise. Together, they tell the story of how a project is actually moving.

The correlation graph is the structure that connects those signals. Instead of storing events in disconnected tables — one for emails, another for tasks, another for retakes — it models the project as a network of relationships. A shot connects to the incidents that affected it. An incident connects to the messages that documented it, the people involved, and the assets implicated.

What this unlocks is a fundamentally different kind of question. Not "what happened?" — which any task tracker can answer — but "why did it happen, what preceded it, and where else might it happen?" A retake that looks like an artistic correction often turns out to be rooted in an email thread that went unanswered four days earlier.

The graph is also institutional memory. Production knowledge — the patterns, the recurring failure modes, the early warning signs — normally lives in the heads of experienced supervisors and evaporates between episodes. By encoding relationships as data, the system retains that knowledge and makes it queryable.

The result is a shift from reactive to anticipatory production management. Problems become visible before they become urgent, root causes become traceable rather than assumed, and the cost of process failures becomes legible for the first time.

Key benefits
Root cause traceability. Trace any retake or delay back through the chain of events that preceded it — across email, Slack, and ShotGrid simultaneously.
Preventive action. A problem on one shot, traced through shared assets or sequences, surfaces risk on shots that haven't opened a retake yet.
Friction mapping. Identify where information flow breaks down — not who failed, but where the process accumulates drag.
Institutional memory. Recurring patterns persist across episodes — the system remembers what experienced supervisors know but can't always articulate.
